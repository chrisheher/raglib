He suggests that myth diminishes the impact of historical
time and is constantly battling with ‘profane’ time, with history, and
with the irreversibility of time and history. This, he argues, is the true
significance of recurrent rituals of purification, of the theme of rebirth,

that collective memory is ahistorical and recognizes only categories and
archetypes, not events and historical figures{ Only the individual is tied
to the concreteness and irreversibility of history.

The human interest in the irreversibility of history and the fascination with 
the new is, according to Eliade, a relatively recent phenomenon. Myths, after all, 
are marked by a continual renewal of the ‘purely’ mythical and sacred epoch and by 
a denial of everyday time. For example, the archaic Oriental tradition of marking 
the end of the year at the same time as the new year is heralded. 

Only cyclical repetition transforms phenomena into real events. ‘Traditional’ 
peoples do not see their own personal existence reflected in the mirror of history. 
Theirs is in fact an anti-historical reality. Eliade maintains that anti-historical 
ideals are still present even where historical time has penetrated mythology—for
example, in the Bible. Prophets, in fact, transfer the ‘pure’ sacred epoch
into the future in the form of eschatological aspirations. People generally look not 
to the past but to a new beginning.
