The transformation of psycho-physiological chaos into social order,
social repression, and the regulating of personal emotions are all 
accomplished mainly through rituals. Rites of passage are particularly 
important in this respect because they link the community and the universe
to birth, naming, initiation into manhood, marriage, changes in social
status such as being raised to the chieftainship or elected as a shaman,
and, finally, to death. Rites of passage normally include a period in
which the individual is alienated from the social structure, a series of
tests, contact with demoniacal forces outside the community, ritual 
purification, and reintegration into the community. As Victor Turner 
demonstrates, the temporary alienation to which the individual is subjected
creates a kind of anti-structure; put another way, the creation of a particular 
(and temporary) confraternity of marginal individuals.”

The most important and typical rite of passage is initiation, which
takes the young man away from the control of his (uninitiated) mother
and sisters and incorporates him into the world of adult hunters; the
young initiate is thus ready for marriage. There are usually physical tests
of endurance, a painful consecration, and the acquisition of tribal
knowledge when myths are performed for the initiates. The initiation
includes a symbolic temporary ‘death’ and contact with the spirits who
show the initiate the way back to the world of the living, where he is
reborn as a man. Temporary death is expressed through various motifs:
the neophyte is swallowed and regurgitated by a monster; a visit to the
netherworld or the underworld; a struggle with spirits; and the conquest
of ritual objects and religious secrets. In brief, the initiation and 
passage to a new status is a wiping away of the old personality and a new
beginning, a cleansing; death and a rebirth that is not, however, really a
resurrection.

It is the paradigm of the ‘new beginning’ that is the basis of initiation, 
and this is often synchronized with seasonal changes and the rituals
that mark them. This is particularly evident, for example, in ritual cycles
like Kunapipi or Mediterranean festivals that mark a dying and resurrected god. 
These rituals thus link the fate of the individual to the general
rhythms of tribal or natural life. Myths, on the other hand, use narrative
 paradigms to uphold social and moral sanctions. Myths about
culture heroes must explain the genesis of the universe and of society,
since heroes shape human society. Their peregrinations thus symbolize
the human life cycle or at least its most important challenges. 

For example, the journeys of the Australian totemic ancestors are completely
expressed in terms of topography, by the ancestor’s journey from his
‘birth’ (emergence) to his ‘death’ (disappearance). Gradually, however,
the culture hero’s biography acquires a paradigmatic significance of its
own as a concatenation of the critical moments in life that are correlated
to rites of passage. Initiations often tend to outweigh and therefore obscure 
all other implications.

Initiation myths are frequently organized around the same principles
as initiation rituals. The most important part of the theme is the tests to
which the hero is subjected in the kingdom of the dead: in heaven, the
home of the sun or sun god, who is often the father of the hero in
American Indians myths; or in an alternative world inhabited by monsters 
and evil spirits. Myths copy the motif found in initiation rituals of a
monster who swallows the hero who is later liberated from the monster’s stomach, 
or they use the motif of a group of children temporarily
kidnapped and imprisoned by an old witch who lives in the forest.

Analogous symbolism drawn from initiations is also found in northern 
Australian myths: the son of one of the Wauwaluk sisters is first
swallowed by the Rainbow Serpent and later regurgitated; Mutjingga
swallows children who are later taken from her stomach. The Kwakiutl
Indians have the motif of being enthralled by a wicked female forest
creature or by a serpent. Jn these examples, however, there is no hero in
the true sense of the word but only the passive subject who submits to
the vicissitudes of the myth or ritual. In myth and archaic folklore in
general, the hero’s part is played only by a mythological personage. His
mythological nature is emphasized by his miraculous birth, ability to talk
