The typical modern way of being religious is, as I have suggested, to be .
a pilgrim, like Momo and Oscar. Modern people are searching, travelling,

Church must offer a pedagogy of freedom which is about more than

discovered to have a shape and meaning. We will only be able to do this if

When St Thomas looked at the moral life, he began by asserting that
we are made in God’s own image, and so we are intelligent and free, and
the source of our own actions.” Becoming virtuous, then, is not about
submitting to external constraints. It is about acting from the very core
of one’s being. It is being auto-mobile, self-moving. We may begin life
feeling that physically we are sports cars and end up feeling like ancient
lorries, but in the moral life, hopefully it is the other way around. When
we think of freedom as choice, then it is something that we have. We
must identify a deeper freedom, which is being who we are. Rabbi Hugo
wrote that in Auschwitz there was a radical transformation of some
of his key values, including that of freedom. He wrote, ‘Freedom 
is something you and I consider that we have, and if you are imprisoned, it is
taken away. But in the camps, freedom became what you were, and this
shaped the attitudes you formed to your situation and your destiny.”

 Virtues are roads to freedom, and our deepest freedom is
 spontaneously to do what is good, because it is what we most deeply desire.
Often one is given the impression that an action is especially virtuous if
it is hard work. That the person who manages to ) resist having another
bottle of wine by an immense effort of will is more virtuous than the
person who happily knows that he has had enough. But that is not what
Aquinas thought. The virtues help us towards the freedom of easily and
effortlessly doing what is good, as a good player can spontaneously
snatch a goal without having to calculate all the angles and trajectories.
His whole body knows what to do. A top-class footballer bends the ball
without hardly thinking about it.

what we most deeply desire. Herbert McCabe wrote, ‘Ethics is entirely
concerned with doing what you want, that is to say with n being free. Most
of the difficulties arise from the difficulty of of recognizing what we want.”
The Ten Commandments are not an external constraint on our freedom:
they tell us who we are. If I feel myself being carried away by a sudden
desire to murder the Prior, then “Thou shalt not kill’ reminds me that I
am his brother, and I do not really want to kill him, much. I would only
feel remorse if I did. Regret is being sorry for what one did in the past.
