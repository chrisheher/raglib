C. The Problem of Classification

We previously described the failure which befell the classification 
of the tale according to themes. Let us make use of our
deductions for a classification according to structural features.

At this point two problems must be singled out: (1) the separation 
of the fairy tale class from other classes of tales; (2) the
classification of fairy tales themselves.

The stability of construction of fairy tales permits a hypothetical 
definition of them which may be stated in the following
way: a fairy tale is a story built upon the proper alternation of
the above-cited functions in various forms, with some of them
absent from each story and with others repeated. By such a
definition the term “fairy” (volsébnyj) loses its sense, since it is
a simple matter for one to imagine a wonderful, fantastic fairy
tale constructed in a totally different way. (Compare certain of
Andersen’s and Brentano’s tales, or Goethe’s tale of the dragon
and the lily, etc.) On the other hand, non-fairy tales may also be
constructed according to the scheme cited. Quite a large number
