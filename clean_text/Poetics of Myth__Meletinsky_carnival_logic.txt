Bakhtin’s work on Rabelais is an important contribution to the
poetics of myth.” The key to understanding the enigmatic Rabelais is
the creativity of the ludic strain in popular culture, which is directly
linked to ancient agrarian festivals that survive in traditional carnivalesque 
rituals. In a class-based society, ritual laughter in popular culture
stands in clear opposition to official culture. It creates a particular world
between the confines of art and life, an anti-clerical world of feasts,
playful parody, and carnivals that is expressed in various rituals and
spectacles, in comedic pieces (verbal and written), and in various genres
of familiar and trivial discourse. In the carnivalesque world it creates a
utopian atmosphere of liberty and equality in which the prevailing social
hierarchy is denied. In other words, there is a return to the Golden Age
of Saturn.

The logic of carnivals is essentially the logic of reversals, of the
 world upside down, of burlesque coronations and dethronings, and of
the substitution of high for Jow and faces for bottoms and vice versa.
Carnivalesque laughter is accessible to everyone. Its festiveness is 
universal and ambivalent. It “buries” and resurrects. It lowers social ideals
to make them more down to earth. Grotesque realism is expressed in a
hyperbolic emphasis on corporeality and a ‘lowness’ that is as destructive 
as it is creative. Carnivals, banquets, battles, free-for-alls, insults,
and curses are joined together in a carnivalesque ‘heli’—-where the
equation between food and death is explicit—that in one sense represents 
all the corporeal ‘lowness’ of human beings elevated, literally and
symbolically, to cosmic significance. The grotesque image of the belly I
devouring and being devoured, as the source of fecundity and rath I
and of putrefaction and death, is a cosmic and universal image. And
behind all these images is the popular concept of an historical and 
collective immortality that is based on the theme of constant renewal.

Bakhtin pays close attention to the realistic symbolism of the carnivalesque 
tradition and its expression in Rabelais, whose “work, if correctly interpreted,
 sheds fight on centuries of the evolution of the popular culture of laughter, 
of which Rabelais is the greatest literary expression.” Not only are carnivalesque 
culture and Rabelais compared, but
the characteristics of carnivalesque poetics are also examined in order to
place in evidence some generalities of mythological consciousness, such
as its symbolism, which is interwoven with a concrete corporeality, and
its unconscious ambivalence. The motifs of the various forms of vital
activity (sexual, alimentary, intellectual, and social) not only interact but
are understood as identical on a symbolic level in exactly the same way
that the microcosm (the human body) is identified with the macrocosm
(the principles that govern nature and the universe). It is precisely from
this parallel that Rabelais draws his hyperbolic and cosmic image of the
human body, based on the opposition of high and low, on journeys inside the body itself, 
and on the theme of body parts that grow and extend themselves even to the point of
 falling off—a theme also found, for example, in Paleo-Asiatic Raven tales—or orifices 
that are hyperbolically open and through which the body ‘makes contact’, 
as it were, with its immediate environment.

Bakhtin’s research demonstrates the manner in which carnivalesque
poetics is based, among Mediterranean people, on cyclical conceptions
of time and on the motif of eternal renewal through death, fertility,
 sacrifice, eroticism, agrarian rituals, and New Year festivities. 
The continuous themes of coronation (elevation) and dethroning (abasement) in
Rabelais’s work—-for example, the burlesque debasing of church bells to
lowly horse harness belis—include the dethroning of King Picrochole,
who is transformed into a servant, and of King Anarche, who is dressed
as a court jester. This is not merely a literary transposition of the
 ‘playking’ theme found in carnivals but a carnivalesque parallel to the rituals
described by Frazer and his followers that renew the regal dignity of the
priest-king—a renewal that sometimes includes humiliation and derision
and sometimes even symbolic death. Carnival traditions and the literature 
connected to them make free use of the ritual motifs of the scapegoat and
