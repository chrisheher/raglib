Hoffmann’s most original aspect is the way in which he imbues
daily life with fantastic qualities. Though the fantastic elements are far
removed from traditional myth, they are nonetheless organized more or
less along the same lines as myth. He blends the ordinary and the fantastic 
into an incredible mixture. Behind ordinary people, things, and situations 
lie fantastic and magical elements, elements that may even be
mythical but treated in such a matter-of-fact way that they become 
ordinary and even comic: the hesitation of Anselmus when faced with a
choice between Serpentina and Veronica corresponds to the struggle

The Romantics ‘new’ mythology—and Hoffmann is particularly coherent in his 
mythological vision—is one of the stages in the process
that led to mythification in the twentieth-century novel. The synthesis of
various mythological traditions, the infinite repetition and twinning of
heroes in space (the Doppelganger) and especially in time (heroes live
forever or die and are resurrected or are reborn in other bodies), and the
primacy of the situation rather than of the character—all these can to a
certain extent define the mythicizing tendencies of the Romantics. Their
tendency to interpret situational contingencies as archetypes explains ‘the
creation of new, quasi-mythological personages. For example, in the
Death of Empedocies, Dionysus himself does not figure in the drama as
a superior being whose intervention resolves the central problem of the
narrative. Instead, the character of Empedacles is presented as an amalgamation 
of traits freely drawn from the archetypes of Christ and Dionysus. 
Even in von Kleist’s Penthestlea, which is peopled with mythical protagonists, 
the core of the work does not concern the personages
