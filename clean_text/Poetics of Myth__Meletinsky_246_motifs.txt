The mythological substratum is also clearly identifiable in tales from
antiquity. Even if earlier I have criticized the ritualists’ views on the
origin of the heroic epic, it must be admitted that the ancient agrarian
civilizations drew freely upon seasonal myths as models that structure
plot and characterization. These have been studied in detail by Gertrude
Levy and Sanskrit scholar Pavel Grincer.™ Grincer identifies a seasonal
mythological complex in Babylonian, Ugaritic, Greek, and Indian epics.
They ali include—apparently arrived at by independent paths—the motifs
 of the miraculous origin of the hero (Gilgamesh, the Pandava, Rama,
Achilles, Keret); the hero’s refusal to become romantically involved with
a goddess (Gilgamesh and Ishtar, Aqhat and Anat, Rama and Shurpanakha, 
Arjuna and Urvashi, Odysseus and Calypso); the hero’s or
heroine’s disappearance caused by death, a fake death, or exile (Aghat,
Rama, the Pandava, Keret, Achilles, Odysseus); the death of the hero’s
ritual substitute (Enkidu, Patroclus, Odysseus’s companions, the
pseudo-Pandava); the kidnapping or attempted kidnapping of the hero’s
consort (Sita, the Draupadi, Helen, Briseis, Keret’s wife, Penelope); the
search for the missing hero or heroine, including a descent to the kingdom 
of the dead (Rama, the Pandava, Gilgamesh, Odysseus); the struggle against 
demoniacal forces (the Rakshasa, Humbaba, and various
marine monsters); and, finally, the reunion with the spouse. Not all these
elements are linked to seasonal rituals. For example, the kidnapping and
return of women is a standard theme in the heroic tales sung by various
Siberian peoples, and they of course do not have anything like agricullure 
and seasonal rituals. And the mortal combat with monsters is not
only part of seasonal rituals but of initiatory rites, as Levy notes, and of
tales dealing with culture heroes. On the whole, however, there can be
little doubt that seasonal rituats are the basic model for the epics of the
classical world. Helen and Sita, for example, are intimately linked to
agrarian myths. Even if many epic heroes seem to derive from historical
prototypes, they come to be associated with one god or another and
their respective functions. The result is that some plots or plot fragments
closely emulate traditional mythologems, which, however, is not irrefutable 
proof that epics all derive from myths and ritual texts,

Although the mythological foundation is discernible in the epics of
antiquity, this genre develops under the relatively stable conditions of
state-level nationalism, which undoubtedly favors the process of demythification. 
Unlike archaic epics, the epics of state-level societies are
based on historical traditions and use the discourse of history to narrate
the events of a remote past that is no longer mythical; to create a
pseudo-history, in other words. ‘Historical’ epics are no more true than
‘mythicai’ epics. They are mostly distinguished by their discourse. The
epic genre thus acquires an ethnic slant by the plethora of geographic
and political references it contains.
