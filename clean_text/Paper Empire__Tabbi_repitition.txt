By positing repetition as a powerful, creative force, both Kierkegaard and
Gaddis try to discard its negative cultural and philosophical image. In this
scheme, reality becomes the repetition of an abstract idea and ideas or, more
specifically, their re-presentation as art, are always only actualizations of the
real. Even though it necessitates a series of repetitions, art is not, as in the
Platonic understanding, merely an “imitation” of life. Its ceaseless effort to
actualize the real by way of re-presentation notwithstanding does not mean
an eternal reproduction of what has been there before. Rather the artist’s
work resembles Kierkegaard’s experience of rereading of the Book of Job:
“Every time I come to it, it is born anew as something original or becomes
new and original in my soul” (205).
