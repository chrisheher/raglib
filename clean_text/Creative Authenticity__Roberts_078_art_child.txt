to your creative expression becomes like raising a child.

Your responsibility is no longer a choice. It won't go away. If you
want it to grow strong and free, you must nourish it. You cherish it, 
though it may often drive you crazy. It will mirror your weaknesses 
to make you grow and show you moments of connection and creativity 
you can only describe as divine. The question of whether you are 
creative enough or talented enough becomes as irrelevant as as 
whether your child is, say, intelligent enough to deserve your attention,

Raising a child creates situations that will constantly test you.
Routines and patterns will constantly be broken to make way
for new ones. So it is with your creative work. Just as some line
of work seems established, you're happy with it, the gallery’s
happy with it and people are buying it, something starts shifting 
inside and doubts creep in. Something else is calling for
attention. Something else wants a say.

We can create a nourishing environment or an abusive one
for ourselves.

But in all this, once we’ve started, we have momentum.

And momentum can carry us forward even when we're confused
about where to go. It’s starting from scratch that’s most difficult.

But with some momentum we’ve got some idea of what to do.

SHOWING UP

Keeping a sketchbook-journal can give us a resource of possibilities.
 No matter how much or how little time we have for
their development, writing down notes or ideas or images that
ome to us is crucial. So is writing them down the moment they
“ome to us. This is crucial for two reasons. First, if we delay, those
ideas will disappear like our dreams in the morning. Two hours
later, what seemed so clear and obvious is gone for good. Second, 
these ideas are our lifeline to our voice. When we give them
attention, they grow stronger. By honoring what pops up, we say
to ourselves that such things matter. If they don’t matter enough
to us to get them down in some form, even some kind of short-
hand, no one else will ever be able to know if they matter to
them. As Emerson wrote, “To believe your own thoughts, to
believe that what is true for you in your private heart is true for
all men—that is genius.”

That’s what calls us to write or draw or paint. Your call is
unique. No life is like yours. No one responds to life the way you
do. Giving precise expression to that personal press of spirit in
the stuff around you—that is “genius”. The word comes from the
Latin meaning “in the spirit of a place”. We give voice to how
our spirit blossoms in the face of something that moves us.
