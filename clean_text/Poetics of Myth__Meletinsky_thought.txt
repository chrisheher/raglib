£52 the Poetics of Myth
Mythology in fact acts as both the battleground and arsenal of the first
forms of religion and poetry, since syncretism is a basic feature of both
religion and poetry. In this sense, mythology is an ancient ideological
expression that is the basis of more developed and differentiated ideological
 expressions. This, however, does not mean that contemporary
philosophy, politics, art, law, and even religion can be directly derived
from myth, nor can alt their attributes be identified with mythical elements,
 as was the case with much twentieth-century speculation. The
historically determined qualitative differences between archaic societies
and modern civilization must be taken into account, notwithstanding the
contradictions particular to civilization. The inseparable bond between
the mythological imagination and its psychological basis and the 
characteristics that are common to all other products of the human 
imagination, cannot be expiained by identifying myth with dreams, 
visions, and products of the unconscious imagination—myth should 
not be reduced to psychology or vice versa. Mythology is much more 
a social and ideological phenomenon than the human imagination as 
such and its social orientation extends well beyond the limits of 
the creations of the collective unconscious. Nor do the lack of 
differentiation and the diffuse quality of mythofogical perception 
allow a facile comparison between the mechanisms of mytho-logic and
scientific thought (as Tylor and Lev Sternberg attempted, for example), 
even if both domains are not completely separate and autonomous (as
 suggested by Lucien Lévy-Bruhl and Ernst Cassirer). Primitive thought 
in some ways does resemble the psychology of infants, but the processes 
that compose the individual psyche and the logical mechanisms of children 
are qualitatively different from the culture of archaic societies.
