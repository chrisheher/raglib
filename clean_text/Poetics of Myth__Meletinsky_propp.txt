The syntagmatic aspect has been examined in detail by Viadimir
Propp in Morphology of the Folktale. Propp concentrates not on myths
but on tales, and folk tales essentially emphasize plot, while the narrative
quality emerges in poetic stereotypes. Propp is undoubtedly one of the
leading figures of structuralism, and Lévi-Strauss’s accusations of formalism 
seem unjustified."* 

After a few experimental attempts in American folkiore studies 
to develop an approach to the syntagmatic aspect of
myth (by Robert Armstrong and John Fischer, for example),'° Alan
Dundes proposed a viewpoint that was critical of Lévi-Strauss 
and applied Propp’s methods to the myths and folk tales of North American
Indians using a terminology partly derived from linguist Kenneth Pyke.”
His starting point is that there is little to distinguish the structure of
myths and tales among North American Indians. Although Dundes has
paid close attention to the synchronic dimension and is far from 
an evolutionist, even the most superficial comparison of his analysis of Indian
tales with Propp’s study of Russian folk tales emphasizes the importance
of a comparative methodology. However, AJ. Greimas, also a structuralist, 
has justified the historical comparison of myths and folk tales."

He has attempted to construct a narrative grammar of myth and blend
the Lévi-Straussian paradigmatic approach with Propp’s emphasis on
the syntagmatic while incorporating the latest findings from logic and
semantics. He applies his synthetic approach to Russian folk tales and to
the Bororo myths cited in Lévi-Strauss’s first volume of Mythologiques.

Greimas reduces the number of Propp’s functions (the general actions 
of the characters in the folk tale) from thirty to twenty. He considers 
every pair joined not only by implication, in the sense that one follows 
the other, but also by logical disjunction, by which he means a
paradigmatic relationship that is independent of the linear development
of the plot. Two pairs of functions are unified by means of a semantic
correlation, negative and positive. The series of negative elements at the
beginning of a tale or a myth must in the end be transformed, usually by
means of a test, into a series of positive elements. In Greimas’s judgment, 
the test is a mediating function that is independent of any particular pair. 

Greimas assigns all functions into three categories: tests; 
adhesion and disengagement; and contractual functions, accepting or 
refusing to perform an action. The fundamental thematic corpus of a tale or
myth is divided into a negative and a positive series that are linked to the
beginning and to the end of the tale, respectively. Every function appears 
in two narrative modes—one truthful, the other deceitful. As Greimas sees it, 
the structure formed by the characters in a tale is a SyStem composed of the 
pairs ‘sender-receiver’ and ‘subject-object’. In the
narrative process, Greimas locates and analyzes the redistribution of
roles, while he links every narrative syntagma to the articulation of the
mythological content in diverse codes. Finally, the diachronic aspects are
compared with the transformations of the underlying content.

Greimas’s logic is impeccable and a few points (for example, the
key role played by tests and the redistribution of roles within tales) are
extremely well argued. But because his arguments are not based on primary texts, 
some of his attempts to construct a new systematic approach
seem forced. For example, sometimes ‘logic’ leads Greimas to pair off
heterogeneous functions or transpose Propp’s very concrete scheme
regarding the subject matter of tales to myths. In myths, there are rarely
hierarchically-arranged series of tests, nor do tales possess the internal
dialectic of alienation or the reestablishing of pacts between protagonists
that typify myths. In brief, while always interesting and methodologically
sound, Greimas’s approach is a bit sketchy.

After having written the above, I received a copy of a book written
by Joseph Courtés, a follower of Greimas.'* The book is a ‘semiotic
reading’ of Lévi-Strauss’s Mythologiques. Courtés argues that LéviStrauss’s 
insistence on the primacy of paradigms, his insistence on homologous codes 
defined not only in terms of structural organization but
in terms of ethnographic ‘meaning’, and the presence of lexical and
mythological confusion (ethnological reality and the structure of oppositions, 
respectively) are obstacles to the full development of his theoties, He proposes
 an addition to Lévi-Straussian methodology in the
form of Greimas’s narrative grammar in order to move Lévi-Strauss’s
structuralism closer to the narrative dimension, thus linking the semantic
structure to syntax by means of a double articulation of adhesion and
disengagement. Courtés believes it is necessary to limit the description
of codes to the background, to snclude a third component between the
two poles of the opposition, to grant a precise thematic role to this third
component (mediator, ‘separator’, ‘attractor’) of oppositions, and to
emphasize the diachronic aspect that is always implicit in Lévi-Strauss.
