No one who reads Malcolm Lowry is
unaware of the autobiographical impulse
and roots to everything he wrote. This
autobiographical poetics, as I think
of it, is one of the many attractions
that Lowry’s oeuvre holds for the
contemporary literary critic and general
reader. But to call Under the Volcano, or
any of the other works, even “Forest Path
to the Spring,” autobiography would bea
mistake. Until the early 1970s, when the
study of life-writing as a theoretically
complex and important genre became
popular, to call a writer autobiographical
could be seen as damning his or her
originality or creativity. However, the
scholarly work of James Olney, Paul John
Eakins, Leigh Gilmore, Sidonie Smith,
and Susanna Egan, among others, has
helped readers appreciate the craft and
sophistication of this genre. In Lowry’s
case, it is safe to say that while he drew
heavily, and at every turn, upon his own
experiences, his voluminous and eclectic
reading, and his personal values, he
polished, modified, and adapted—in
short, transformed—these sources into
the pure gold of narrative creation.
Telling one’s life story and telling it
well, Lowry knew, required as much
aesthetic skill, artistic control, and
imagination as concocting fictions out
of thin air. His characters never merely
equal Lowry the man, but they do
embody, represent, and speak to his
intensely private fears, sufferings, guilts,
beliefs, and joys.

This autobiographical poetic extends
to include Lowry’s quotation of himself—
from his own letters to others, from
letters written to him, from his actual
conversations, detailed diaries and
notebooks, as well as from his other
work—and characterizes his obsession
with and appropriation of others’ works.
Like so many writers before him, from
Milton to T. S. Eliot, Lowry cannibalized
what he read and made it Hisown. But
unlike other writers, he was also horrified
by this capacity for appropriation.
He plagued himself with specters of
plagiarism and chastised himself for
succumbing to “hysterical identifications”
with other writers. In this so-called
plagiarism and identification he
recognized a personal capacity for
exploitation that he saw magnified in
Mexican history and postwar politics . . .
and it haunted him.

In the last analysis, however, the
quality of Under the Volcano that keeps
drawing me back is the amazing beauty
of its prose. The themes encourage me
to reflect; the complexities of allusion
provoke me to ask questions and
pursue elusive details (and I always find
something new on each reading); the
autobiographical poetic never ceases to
surprise and delight me with some fresh
artistic challenge skillfully negotiated. p>
