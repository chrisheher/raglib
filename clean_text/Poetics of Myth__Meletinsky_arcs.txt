If tragedy is eschatology transformed into ethics, the lyric genre is
nature personified as man. Freidenberg delineates the particular archaic
character of the Greek lyric form, which reflects not only its nearness to
its mythological roots but also the lack of differentiation between subject 
and object. The poet still identifies himself with the god. Heroic and
sacred myths become the personal biographies of the poets. The most
elementary experience is reified into an objectively defined reality, even
using the third person point of view. The concreteness of the image is
presented in conventional form, yet the basis of comparisons is no
longer mythological but metaphorical.

In sum, despite many debatable points and an awkward and sometimes unreadable 
styie, Freidenberg makes a solid contribution to understanding mythopoesis 
by her analysis of the development of various genres as a result of changes 
within the semantic paradigms of the myths of antiquity. Because of her 
materialist orientation, she is able to integrate the work of Usener,
 Lévy-Bruhl, and Cassirer into an historical approach that focuses on 
the development of mythopoesis and literature.

Unlike Freidenberg, Jacob Golosovker, in Logic of Ancient Myth,
ignores the problem of the historical development of myth in order to
concentrate on its poetic and aesthetic logic and on the systematic 
features of the mythological imagination™ Like Lévy-Bruhl, Golosovker
demonstrates how myths consistently ignore the laws of formal logic,
especially that of the fertium non datur. Mythical logic, in his opinion,
starts from mistaken assumptions in order to reach a desired conclusion.
In myth, everything that is normally hypothetical is represented as categorical, 
and the independent and dependent variables are interchanged.
The free play of the aesthetic imagination and of the creativity of human
aspirations leads to the emergence of the ‘miraculous’ myth, its complete 
disregard for the qualities and characteristics of beings and objects,
its tendency to transform anything and everything, its casual taking for
granted of everything that is mysterious, and its shrouding of the 
commonplace with an air of mystery. Yet Golosovker is opposed to a sub-
jective psychological interpretation of the mythopoetical imagination, in
which everything is real and every poetic image is made corporeal. The
aesthetics of myth have their own objectivity and their own ontology.

Golosovker seems to be anticipating a Lévi-Straussian structuralist
approach when he affirms, in contrast to Lévy-Bruhi, that mythological
thought is a creative cognitive process with its own logic. He seems to
be very close to some contemporary theories of the primacy of the
imagination with respect to experience in myth when he argues that the
mythic image is not a representation but a signified because in myth the
concrete object is transformed into a symbol.™ Golosovker traces the
movement of a few sensory images along a ‘significance curve and
shows how in myth this curve becomes transformed into a circle. This
circle is the result of a particular logic of combination and transformation—
the “multi-dimensional scale of combination”, in his words—that
is similar to Lévi-Strauss’s ideas on syntagmatic chains. For example,
Golosovker examines the visual point of view of Greek myths. On the
horizontal axis, there is a shift from the one-eyed Cyclops (a unilateral
view) to the many-eyed Argus (who can see in all directions), to the
divine and all-seeing Helios, and to the penetrating vision of the Argonauts’ 
helmsman, Lynceus. On the vertical axis, which represents blindness, 
there is a progressive inversion, from blindness toward the outside
world to blindness toward the self. Thus Oedipus moves from the condition
 of a metaphorical ‘blindness’ toward prophecies regarding his fate
to the prophetic powers of blindness, whereas Tiresias develops in the
opposite direction. At the same time, the myths contrast ‘interior insight’
 and the poet’s rich imagination to the false wisdom of the mad
orgiast, the disbelief that accompanies a blindness toward prophetic visions
 to blind faith. The large semantic circle includes the Cyclops, Argus, 
Helios, Lynceus, Oedipus, Pentheus, Cassandra and Licurgus,
Daphnis, Phoenix, Phineus, Merope, Orion, Tiresias, and Oedipus—the
cycle comes full circle.** Golosovker is almost casual in his observation
that while contradictions are not resolved in myth, dilemmas come to a
happy conclusion as a result of synthesis. He thus anticipates the theory
