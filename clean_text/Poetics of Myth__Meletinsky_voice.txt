Freidenberg’s ‘universal law of semanticizing’ is also noteworthy.
She argues that semantics goes well beyond empirical reality. For example, 
the words ‘king’ and ‘slave’ existed before the development of
monarchy or slavery. Her arguments on the role of rhythm in myth and
ritual are also suggestive. She claims that rhythm transforms a concrete
act into a mimetic gesture and the word into rhythmic discourse, which
of course is the embryonic form of poetry and prose. In her analysis of
the semanticizing of rhythm, Freidenberg examines the rhythmic quality
of time and space. Her starting point is the identity between some parts
of the body and the cosmos and also the use of the dichotomy high/low
(raising and lowering one’s voice, for example) as the semantic and 
interpretative base of metrical poetry.

Freidenberg’s monograph /mage and Concept, subtitied An Essay
on Historical Poetics, studies the origins of archaic literature, which had
no antecedent tradition on which to base itself except for myth and ritual
—that is, verbal forms and semanticized objects.’ She examines in
mythological images the first steps toward abstraction and the incipient
division that emerged between object and property, space and time,
cause and effect, self and others, the subject and object of cognition, and
active and passive modes. The mythic image thus becomes poetic, and
‘pre-metaphors’ are transformed into real metaphors. Freidenberg argues that 
‘artistic’ concepts developed as the forms taken by mythological images. 
The metaphor emerges with the transformation of concrete
to abstract meaning, with the result that the metaphor gives rise to 
conceptual figurativeness. When the properties of an object are distinguished 
from the object itself in this process of transformation, the subJect continues 
to define itself according to the object, and the abstraction
comes to signify the concrete. For example, suffering comes to be seen
as a kind of illness. The mythological perception of phenomena as two
opposed hypostases is conserved by the structural aspect of the semantic aspect 
of the ‘concept’. The ‘concept’, however, infringes upon these
twin identities and introduces the qualitative opposition between reality
and appearance, between the object as it ‘really’ exists and its aspects
that merely resemble reality. Mimesis in the classical world is, according
