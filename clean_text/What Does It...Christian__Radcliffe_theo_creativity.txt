How different can two believers be for the encounter to be fertile? This
is a complex question which we do not need to explore here. Of course
ultimately we must share orthodoxy, but this is not to narrow the scope
of the conversation; it is to enter the broad terrain of the mystery, in
which we are liberated from the tightness of ideology. It is a serious
misuse of language to use the word ‘orthodox’ to mean conservative or,
even worse, rigid. Orthodoxy does not lie in the unvarying and thoughtless 
repetition of received formulas. As Karl Rahner pointed out, that can
be a form of heresy. Orthodoxy is speaking about our faith in ways that
keep open the pilgrimage towards the mystery. Often it is hard to know
immediately whether a new statement of belief is a new way of stating
our faith or its betrayal. It takes time for us to tell. When Chou en Lai was
asked if the French Revolution was a success, he replied that it was too
soon to know!

It is a failure of courage to rush into condemnation. When Marie-
Joseph Lagrange OP founded the Ecole Biblique in 1890 in Jerusalem
and began critical historical studies of the Old Testament, showing that
it was not always to be read literally, then the fury of the Holy Office fell
upon him and he was condemned. Years later it has become accepted
that what he said was perfectly orthodox and become universally
accepted in the Catholic Church. Fear never serves the pursuit of truth.
It is the task of our guardians of orthodoxy to ensure that panic never
suppresses reflection, to have the courage to stop premature condemnation, 
to ensure that we take the time we need.

Even if someone says something which is clearly unorthodox, my first
reaction must be to see what truth they are trying to say rather than
immediately condemn their error. They may be struggling to say something true, 
even if they are putting it in a way that is untrue. If a Christian asserts that 
Jesus is just another manifestation of God, like Krishna,
then I believe that he is wrong. This is incompatible with our faith. But
maybe he is trying badly to say something true which I need to heed.
Noel O’Donoghue once described heresy as ‘trapped light. One must
