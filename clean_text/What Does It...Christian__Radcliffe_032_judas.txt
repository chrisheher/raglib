The Jewish tradition maintained that ‘whereas the slaves eat

That final meal offers us successive steps into an ever deeper freedom.
First of all we shall look at the betrayal of Jesus, his loss of freedom.
Then we shall reflect briefly on how Jesus transcends victimhood. Then there
is his freedom of choice, which is the ordinary and basic freedom of
human beings. But the Last Supper invites us to deeper freedoms, the
freedom of spontaneity, and ultimately the freedom of giving away our
lives.

Betrayal

We begin with betrayal. Why did Judas hand over Jesus to death? We do —
not know the answer. When Jesus met him in the garden he asked him,
‘Friend, why are you here?’ (Matthew 26.50). Judas did not reply. It is the
question which is repeated in the reproaches of Good Friday: ‘Oh, my
people, what have I done to you? How have I offended you? Answer me?
We cannot. Evil is absurd and nonsensical. Faced with the death of Jesus,
all that we can do is to put the same question to him: ‘Friend, why are
you here?’ The mystery of evil has no explanation, but it is swallowed up
in the more profound mystery of good.
