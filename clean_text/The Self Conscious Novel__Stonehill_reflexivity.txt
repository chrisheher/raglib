To answer our question at the start of this chapter, we now see that mot
any story is suitable for the mode of self-consciousness. This list makes it
clear that only those narratives that are in some sense about themselves as
well as about their ostensible subjects will be endowed appropriately by
the features we have identified. Fictions that tell their stories without
directly confronting their own ontology will not draw extensively from
this repertoire.

The self-conscious novel bears a particular set of relations to writer,
reader, and real world that distinguishes it from other novels. The 
intellectual, emotional, and esthetic effect of the self-conscious novel’s 
insistence upon its own artifice is fundamentally and demonstrably different
from that of a novel which preserves its illusion of life inviolate. It is in
fact a distinct and legitimate genre. Its repertoire of narrational, stylistic,
structural, characterizational, and thematic techniques is broad yet capable of 
definition. Additionally, the self-conscious novel is, as a genre,
the inheritor and result of a specific literary tradition. Its historical 
development will be explored in the chapters that follow.
