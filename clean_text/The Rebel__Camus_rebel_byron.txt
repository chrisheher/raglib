farewell fear, farewell remorse... . Evil, be thou my
good.” It is the cry of outraged innocence.

The romantic hero, therefore, considers himself compelled to do 
evil by his nostalgia for an unrealizable good.
Satan rises against his Creator because the latter employed
force to subjugate him. ‘““Whom reason hath equal’d,”’ says
Milton’s Satan, “force hath made supreme above his
equals.” Divine violence is thus explicitly condemned. The
rebel flees from this aggressive and unworthy God, “Farthest 
from him is best,” and reigns over all the forces hostile 
to the divine order. The Prince of Darkness has only
chosen this path because good is a notion defined and
utilized by God for unjust purposes. Even innocence irritates
 the Rebel in so far as it implies being duped. This
“dark spirit of evil who is enraged by innocence” creates
