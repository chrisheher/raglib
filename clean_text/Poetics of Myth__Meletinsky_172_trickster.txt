I North America is the home of the classic trickster, distant precursor
of Renaissance literature.’ Tricksters are astute and cunning because
primitive thought does not distinguish between intellect and magic. It is
when mythological consciousness clarifies the difference between intelligence 
and cleverness, trickery and honesty, and social organization and
chaos that the personage of the trickster emerges as a counterpart to the
culture hero. Unlike a malicious hero of Oceanic mythology such as
Maui, for example, Raven and other similar figures from Siberian and
North American mythology use not only cunning but deploy treat trickery. 
These tricks are parodies that correspond to the serious acts of
shamans or culture heroes. They even have their own burlesque and
degraded versions of sacred rites. Tricksters constantly emphasize the
low instincts such as gluttony and licentiousness that lead them to ar
social acts like incest and stealing a family’s food supply. Sometimes
Indians distinguish two different characters of the same name, a culture
hero and a trickster such as Raven or Coyote, for example, but this is
not universal and is in any case secondary. It is no accident that two of
the names of the Great Hare are Gitiskap and Wisaka, which means
‘two-faced’.

An ambiguous personage like the culture hero/demiurge/trickster
blends in a single person the pathos of the cosmic and social order on
the one hand and, on the other, the manifestation of a harmony that is
not yet fully constituted, an expression of disorganization, in other
words. This contradiction is possible because mythological cycfes link
events to the mythical past, to the time before ngid, universal orderliness 
was established. Yet it must be remembered that the actions of the
culture hero’s negative Doppelgdnger (for example, To Karvuvu in
Melanesia) or of a trickster like Raven are themselves paradigmatic

North America also has its complement of twin heroes, who sometimes 
fight but occasionally collaborate.” Among the Iroquois some
survivals of rival culture heroes are preserved in the figures of 
Teharonhyawagon, a creator, sky god, and agricultural divinity, and his
demoniacal twin Tarongaskaron. Among the tribes of the southwestern
Plains, the couple does not consist of rival demiurges but of heroic
vagabonds who destroy the monsters that menace peace and harmony.
This area is the home of a widespread cycle of myths centered on
Lodge-Boy and Thrown-Away or on a variant, Young-Man-Abandonedin-a-Brook.
 They are sons of a woman killed by an evil spirit. One is
abandoned in a wigwam, the other in a bush or brook. Later, the father
finds the ‘savage’ Thrown-Away arid ‘domesticates’ him. The twins
begin their careers as traveling monster-slayers and confront a giant
cannibal, a gigantic moose, a thunderbird, a rolling stone, a man-eating
antelope, and a giant beaver, among others. Among the Navaho, the
heroic twins are the grandchildren of the first humans and the children of
the Sun. They must kill various monsters to allow people to multiply
and the tribe to migrate to its present-day home.

Twins are also extremely important in South American mythology,
especially among Araucanian-speaking tribes, Tupi-Guarani speakers,
and Carib-speaking peoples: Keri and Kame among the Bakairi,
Makunaima and Pighe (Pia) among Carib speakers, and Bakororo and
Itubore among the Bororo. These twins are often represented as the II
sons of the creator or of the main culture hero, whose wife was killed by
a jaguar or a man-eating beast; in a few versions—the Bororo, for example
—the twins are Jaguar’s sons. Once they become aware of their
special origin, the twins kill jaguars and demons and, in the end, 
transform themselves into the sun and the moon, A similar theme is found in
the Popol-Vuh, the mythological epos of the Quiché of Central America.
Ahpu and Shbalanke are born of magic fruit or from the heads of ancestors. 
Besides fighting heroically against hellish demons, they act as culture heroes;
 for example, they introduce maize to humankind.

The rich mythology of Mexican peoples, such as the Toltec, Aztec,
and, in part, Maya, is linked to an extremely complex model of the 
universe as well as to a cyclical scheme of cosmic epochs. At the heart of
the system is Quetzalcoatl, the plumed serpent, who functions mostly as
a demiurge and culture hero. He creates humans (or, more correctly, he
re-creates them from bones he steals from the netherworld) and steals
maize seed from the rightful owner, the Red Ant. Quetzalcoatl also has
a hand in the fashioning of the universe from the limbs of an earth goddess 
whom he defeated. The role of culture hero is even more obvious
