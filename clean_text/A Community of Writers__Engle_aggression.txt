

On the one hand Vance was very much a formalist. In work he talked a lot 
about technique, about what worked and what didnt. In his own writing he 
liked to set himself technical probje ns, judging that the solving of such 
problems could lead to high art. He had certain technical axioms that he 
challenged us with. In a really successful tense dramatic scene the combatants 
per talk about exactly the real issue between them. We read our stuff aloud. 
If you couldn’t read it convincingly, it wasn’t right. There had to be a powerful 
consonance been sound and sense. If the style created its own autonomous epic, 
then it was mere virtuosity. If on the other hand it submitted to dogged narration 
and exposition, then it had been sold out.


That was a feeling I got from Vance, that he felt better if he had
thick country shoes on or boots with some mud on the soles or SOW
cow shit, that he liked a house with a certain quantity of dog haiy
from Bix or Moondog, that he wanted to own some land, and ming of good things. 
Milk and semen. Vance kept it light, But there was nothing show-off or
phoney about it. And there was certainly no hint of the oppressive.
Vance was not saying to us, be like me, write like me. He was only
saying that for him the composition of a self and of a text go hand
in hand. That defined a position. It gave you a sort of star fix to navigate 
by as you came to your own decisions.
