Even in areas where totemism as a social and religious institution is
a mere survival, totemic systems of classification linger and mythical
heroes are given animal names. The totemic hypostasis of the mythological
 hero expresses his role as a mediator. His symbolic role in the
resolution of fundamental contradictions is established by the logic of
the narrative.

Aranda myths, as well as those of other tribes of central Australia,
describe in detail the travels of the totemic ancestors as they seek out
their kin or as they hunt, sometimes at the head of a group of young
people. While journeying, the ancestors stop, eat, and perform rituals.
Tired at the end of their trip, they go underground, underwater, or are
swallowed by various rocks. These points become totemic or sacred
sites where contemporary Aranda initiate young men and perform the
rituals of inticiuma, the magical multiplication of the animals of their
particular totem. During the rituals, the myths of the Dreamtime ancestors 
are re-enacted. Every main point in the ancestors’ journey is a kind
of monument to their creative activity. During their travels, the ancestors 
create objects and cultural mores, though this is a secondary function among
 Australian peoples. For example, totemic ancestors such as
wildcat and fly-lizard help complete the creation of human larvae, who
are lying unable to move on rocks in the water. They circumcise them to
make them fully human, teach them the art of making fire and cooking,
divide humans into two phratries, and give them spears and boomerangs
and each a personal churinga that functions as a receptacle for the soul.
The ancestor of the gray kangaroos steal fire from the body of a giant
kangaroo, whereas the eagle-hawk totemic ancestor shows humans how
to make and use stone axes.”

Besides these totemic ancestors, the myths of the northern and
southeastern Aborigines include stories of ‘supertotemic’ mythical 
heroes who are shared by more than one totemic group; every part of the
hero’s body corresponds to a particular totemic group, for example. In
the southeast, there is the image of the universal-father who lives in the
heavens. He is a cuiture hero and the patron of initiation rites. He has
several names: Nurundere, Koin, Biral, Nurelli, Bunjil, Baiame, and Daramulun. 
‘Bunjil’, for example, means ‘eagle with wedge-shaped tail’
and refers to one of the phratries of the Kulin tribe. Sometimes their
myths mention Palian, who is personified as a bat or a crow, the rather
sordid and stupid twin of Bunjil. The two are, inevitably, always at odds.
Invather characters of the same stripe, the genetic link with the totemic
ancestors is even more veiled, although the denomination as ‘father’
evokes the concept of ancestor.

Even though these protagonists are strictly linked to the mythical
past, they typically continue their existence today in the heavens and not
on the earth itself." This is an embryonic sky mythology and the next
step to full apotheosis. The All-Father not only introduces initiation
rituals but continues in some sense to control them today. Tokarev notes
that this typical central-Australian figure embodies the archaic conception 
of a frightening spirit, a sort of bogey-man (only the non initiated
believe in him) who transforms hovs into ments ‘father’ is above all
a culture hero and, in some ways, a demturge. Along with his mother
(Emu), Daramulun plants trees, gives humans their jaws, and defines
initiation rituals. Baiame comes from the northeast with his two wives,
creates some people from wood and clay, transforms animals into humans, 
and gives them their laws and customs. According to a Wiradthuri
myth, Daramulun also wanders about in search of wild honey.”

On the basis that a sky-god was tentatively identified among the
northern Aborigines (Nogamajn for the Murinbata, for example) and the
central Australian Aborigines, even one with rather amorphous characteristics 
(such as Atnatu among the Aranda), a few scholars such as Eliade have suggested 
that this figure is the oldest and most universal divinity, older even than the
 totemic ancestors of the Dreamtime. Eliade compares the relationship of these
 sky-heroes with the totemic ancestors to the link between various divine
generations in other mythologies, for example, Tiamat and Marduk and Uranus and Zeus.

One of the suggested proofs for the relationship between skyheroes and totemic ancestors 
and between different divine generations is the particular role of these mythical 
characters in the initiation of shamans, who are in turn particularly important as 
mediators between heaven and earth. After the separation of the sky from the earth, 
men became mortal, and shamans from that moment on act as intermediaries
between the two domains.” They are said to fly and can climb to heaven
by scaling a tall tree or using a magic rope. Among the Australian Aborigines, 
the relationship of these sky-heroes ta shamanism and their initiatory rites 
in reality seems to be linked to the late development of a
celestial mythology, since the type of initiation ceremony that all tribes
possess is clearly older than the complex of beliefs and rituals linked to
the choosing of shamans.”
