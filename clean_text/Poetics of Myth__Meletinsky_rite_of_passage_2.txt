Stanner believes that Australian Aboriginal ‘philosophy’, ingenuous

He examines initiation rites for hints of the concept of sacrifice. The

cruel and arduous tests. In fact, ‘suffering’ is perhaps not even an 
applicable concept in this case because individual well-being is irrelevant for
myth and folklore. The transposition of the link between sacrifice and
initiation to the dimension of the mythological hero’s personality is a
characteristic of relatively modern folklore.

In the South American myth of the bird-nester found among the
Bororo and neighboring groups, the hero commits incest and is cruelly
punished by his father.’ Only a miracle can save him from the severe
tests to which he is subjected. But his father’s (or father-in-law’s) 
behavior breaks the norms that govern ‘kinship’ relations and thus causes the
father’s own death. The hero passes the tests and becomes a culture
hero and the tribe’s benefactor. An extreme example of this is Prometheus, 
especially in Aeschylus’s version, in which Prometheus is presented 
as a rebel and martyr: he is chained to a rock and tormented by
an eagle.

The Passion-like quality of the suffering hero is even more evident
in cult myths of dying and resurrected gods such as Osiris and Adonis,
who are innocent but necessary victims. The culmination of the genre is
obviously Christ, the Savior who obeys His father and voluntarily suffers
torments and death. Ail these mythical protagonists—culture heroes and.
suffering gods, saviors, and the prophets of various religions like Christ
and Buddha—are paradigmatic figures for later generations. Their
greatness and importance have little to do with individual psychology
but derive from universal human values. This is a point worth keeping in
mind when examining the neo-mythological world view, which is geared
to the psychology of the individual personality.

THE SEMANTICS OF MYTHICAL PLOTS AND SYSTEMS

It is obvious that numerous themes and motifs are repeated in many
mythologies: the creation of the world from an egg or the body of a
chthonic being who has been killed; {the emergence of the world from a
primordial ocean; the flood; the separation of sky and earth, the cosmic
tree; the final finishing touches given to incomplete human beings, the
stealing of fire; the incest of the founders of the lineage; and, finally,
combat with chthonic monsters. Most of these universal motifs are associated with the cosmogonic sphere, which confirms the fundamental
importance of cosmogony in mythology taken as a whole. Yet
‘universality’ is in fact not absolute. It is most evident only in the welldeveloped mythologies of ancient civilizations. These motifs and themes
do not exhaust the cosmic model, nor are they a complete representa~
tion of the total functions of the mythological model of the world considered as a total, modeling sign system.”
